Conventions and Reference List

  • Tropical Geometry and Mirror Symmetry by Mark Gross. Chapter 1 of this book is the main reference.

  • "First Steps in Tropical Geometry" by Richter-Gerbert, Sturmfels, Theobald. Gross says chapter 1 of his book mostly follows this reference. I looked at it a few times while prepping these notes.

  • Introduction to Tropical Geometry by Maclagan and Sturmfels. Used to cross-reference the more opaque definitions in Gross's book. This is a little more down-to-earth and typically provides enlightening examples.

Parameterized Tropical Curves

We skip over the basics of tropical polynomials, tropical curves in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn and fans and go straight to section 1.3 of Gross's book, where we first encounter generalized tropical curves built from the data of weighted graphs and marked graphs. I gave a (bad) introduction to cones and fans here, but the unfamiliar reader should either check out section 1.2 of Gross's book or look at Fulton's Introduction to Toric Varieties. Note that any tropical curve V(f)V(f)V(f) locally looks like a fan around every vertex after translating to the origin.

Balancing condition

We first need to discuss the balancing condition. Let Σ⊂Rn\Sigma \subset \mathbb{R}^nΣ⊂Rn be a one-dimensional rational fan; i.e. a fan comprised only of the origin and a collection of sss rays. Let viv_ivi​ be the first lattice point of the iiith ray in Σ\SigmaΣ. We give Σ\SigmaΣ the structure of a weighted fan by assigning a positive integer mi∈Zm_i \in \mathbb Zmi​∈Z to viv_ivi​, and we say that Σ\SigmaΣ is balanced if

∑mivi=0. \sum m_i v_i = 0.∑mi​vi​=0.

Before we generalize this, let's review some terminology.

  • A polyhedron P⊂RnP\subset \mathbb{R}^nP⊂Rn is the intersection of finitely many closed half-spaces.

  • A face of a polyhedron PPP is determined by a linear functional λ∈(Rn)∨\lambda \in \mathbb (\mathbb{R}^n)^\veeλ∈(Rn)∨ like so. (Note that the codimension 1 faces of PPP occur on the of a single supporting hyperplane, the codimension 2 faces occur on the intersection of two supporting hyperplanes, etc.)

face⁡λ(P)={x∈P ∣ λ(x)≤λ(y) for all y∈P}. \operatorname{face}_\lambda(P) = \{x \in P ~\mid~ \lambda(x) \leq \lambda(y) \text{ for all } y \in P\}.faceλ​(P)={x∈P ∣ λ(x)≤λ(y) for all y∈P}.

  • A polyhedral complex Σ\SigmaΣ is a collection of polyhedra such that

    • if P∈ΣP\in \SigmaP∈Σ then every face of PPP is also in Σ\SigmaΣ and

    • if P,Q∈ΣP, Q\in \SigmaP,Q∈Σ then P∩Q∈ΣP\cap Q \in \SigmaP∩Q∈Σ.

    The polyhedra of Σ\SigmaΣ are called cells. Cells which aren't faces of any larger cell are called facets. Notice that all the facts need not have the same dimension, which is why...

  • ...we say that Σ\SigmaΣ is pure of dimension ddd if every facet of Σ\SigmaΣ has the same dimension.

  • The affine span of a polyhedron PPP is the smallest affine subset of Rn\mathbb R^nRn which contains PPP. The relative interior of PPP is the interior of PPP inside its affine span.

We can now extend the balancing condition to arbitrary fans.

Definition 3: Balancing Condition   Let Σ\SigmaΣ be a rational fan in Rn\mathbb R^nRn, pure of dimension ddd, and suppose we have a weighting function mmm which assigns weights m(σ)∈Nm(\sigma) \in \mathbb Nm(σ)∈N for all the maximal cones σ\sigmaσ. Given τ∈Σ\tau \in \Sigmaτ∈Σ of dimension d−1d-1d−1, let LLL be the linear space parallel to τ\tauτ. The abelian group LZ=L∩ZnL_\mathbb{Z} = L\cap \mathbb Z^nLZ​=L∩Zn is free of rank d−1d-1d−1 since τ\tauτ is a rational cone, and then N(τ)=Zn/LZ≅Zn−d+1N(\tau) = \mathbb Z^n/L_\mathbb Z \cong \mathbb Z^{n-d+1}N(τ)=Zn/LZ​≅Zn−d+1.

Now, for each maximal cone σ\sigmaσ with τ⊊σ\tau \subsetneq \sigmaτ⊊σ, the set (σ+L)/L(\sigma +L)/L(σ+L)/L is a one-dimensional cone in N(τ)⊗ZRN(\tau)\otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb RN(τ)⊗Z​R – it's just the projection of σ\sigmaσ onto LLL. ∑m(σ)vσ=0,\sum m(\sigma) v_\sigma = 0,∑m(σ)vσ​=0,

iterating over all σ\sigmaσ containing τ\tauτ. We say that the fan Σ\SigmaΣ is balanced if it's balanced at all codimension (d−1d-1d−1)-cones.

If we instead have a rational polyhedral complex Σ\SigmaΣ of pure dimension ddd with weights m(σ)∈Nm(\sigma)\in \mathbb Nm(σ)∈N on all maximal cells, then for each τ∈Σ\tau \in \Sigmaτ∈Σ the fan star⁡Σ(τ)\operatorname{star}_{\Sigma}(\tau)starΣ​(τ) inherits the weighting function mmm. We therefore say that Σ\SigmaΣ is balanced if star⁡Σ(τ)\operatorname{star}_{\Sigma}(\tau)starΣ​(τ) is balanced for all (d−1)(d-1)(d−1)-dimensional cells τ\tauτ.

Tropical curves from graphs

We can use the balancing condition to define tropical curves in a more abstract setting than in Rn\mathbb R^nRn. Let Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ be a connected graph with no bivalent vertices (i.e. no vertices with exactly two connected edges). We denote by Γ‾[0]\overline{\Gamma}^{[0]}Γ[0] and Γ‾[1]\overline{\Gamma}^{[1]}Γ[1] the set of vertices and edges respectively of Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ. It's convenient to freely switch back and forth between thinking of Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ as the purely combinatorial object defined by Γ‾[0]\overline{\Gamma}^{[0]}Γ[0] and Γ‾[1]\overline{\Gamma}^{[1]}Γ[1] and as a topological space given a union of closed line segments.

Denote by Γ‾∞[0]\overline{\Gamma}^{[0]}_\inftyΓ∞[0]​ the set of univalent vertices of Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ, and write

Γ=Γ‾∖Γ‾∞[0].\Gamma = \overline{\Gamma} \setminus \overline{\Gamma}^{[0]}_\infty.Γ=Γ∖Γ∞[0]​.

Thinking of Γ\GammaΓ and Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ as topological spaces, we see that Γ\GammaΓ is a "graph with some non-compact edges", i.e. "legs" or "half-edges" and Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ is its closure. This explains the notation, especially if we consider the univalent vertices of Γ‾\overline{\Gamma}Γ to occur at infinity.

Here are some rapid-fire definitions.

  • We let Γ∞[1]\Gamma^{[1]}_\inftyΓ∞[1]​ denote the non-compact edges of Γ\GammaΓ.

  • A flag of Γ\GammaΓ is a vertex/edge pair (V,E)∈Γ[0]×Γ[1](V,E)\in\Gamma^{[0]}\times \Gamma^{[1]}(V,E)∈Γ[0]×Γ[1] with V∈EV \in EV∈E.

  • A weight function is a map assigning positive integer weights to the edges of Γ\GammaΓ:

w:Γ[1]→N.w:\Gamma^{[1]} \to \mathbb N.w:Γ[1]→N.

  • A marked graph is a tuple (Γ,x1,...,xk)(\Gamma, x_1, ..., x_k)(Γ,x1​,...,xk​) where Γ\GammaΓ is as above and x1,...,xkx_1,...,x_kx1​,...,xk​ are labels assigned to the non-compact edges with weight 000, i.e. {Ex1,...,Exk}⊆Γ∞[1]\{E_{x_1},...,E_{x_k}\} \subseteq \Gamma^{[1]}_\infty{Ex1​​,...,Exk​​}⊆Γ∞[1]​ is precisely the set of non-compact edges such that w(Exi)=0w(E_{x_i}) = 0w(Exi​​)=0.
