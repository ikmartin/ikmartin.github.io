Some notes on actions of algebraic groups

I'm trying to understand the localization formula for equivariant Chow groups. This requires a working knowledge of algebraic groups and especially of linear algebraic groups, so I'm reading the notes "Introduction to actions of algebraic groups" by Michel Brion to get up to speed. Not coincidentally this is the same author of the paper "Equivariant Chow groups and torus actions".

  1. Algebraic Groups
    1. Actions on Varieties

Algebraic Groups

All algebraic varieties are varieties over C\mathbb CC, and they have the Zariski topology instead of the complex topology. The algebra of regular functions on a variety is denoted by OX(X)\mathcal O_X(X)OX​(X). We start with a definition and then some examples.

Definition: (Algebraic Group).   An algebraic group is a variety GGG equipped with the structure of a group such that the multiplication map μ:G×G→G, (g,h)↦gh \mu:G\times G\to G, ~ (g,h)\mapsto gh μ:G×G→G, (g,h)↦gh and the inverse map ι:G→G, g↦g−1\begin{aligned} \iota: G\to G, ~ g\mapsto g^{-1} \end{aligned}ι:G→G, g↦g−1​ are morphisms of varieties.

The neutral component of an algebraic group GGG is the connected component G0⊆GG^0\subseteq GG0⊆G containing the identity element (called the "neutral element" by Brion). This is also called the identity component.

Basically all the algebraic groups I'm interested in are linear algebraic groups; subsets of GL⁡n\operatorname{GL}_nGLn​.

Example:    Here are several examples of algebraic groups.

  1. All finite groups are algebraic groups.

  2. The general linear group GL⁡n\operatorname{GL}_nGLn​ is the subset of all n×nn\times nn×n matrices, which as a variety is Cn2\mathbb C^{n^2}Cn2. It is the inverse image of the determinant map det⁡:Cn2→C\operatorname{det}:\mathbb C^{n^2} \to \mathbb Cdet:Cn2→C away from 000; hence it is an open set and is an affine variety. Matrix multiplication is a polynomial in the coefficients of the matrices, and is hence a regular map. Likewise, inversion A↦A−1A\mapsto A^{-1}A↦A−1 is a polynomial in the coefficients of of AAA and 1/det⁡(A)1/\operatorname{det}(A)1/det(A). Thus GL⁡n\operatorname{GL}_nGLn​ is an algebraic group.

  3. Any Zariski-closed subgroup of GL⁡n\operatorname{GL}_nGLn​ is also an algebraic group, for instance, SL⁡n\operatorname{SL}_nSLn​. We call such groups linear algebraic groups.

  4. The multiplicative group C∗\mathbb C^*C∗ is an affine algebraic group. It is a linear algebraic group since C∗≅GL⁡1\mathbb C^* \cong \operatorname{GL}_1C∗≅GL1​.

  5. The algebraic torus Tn=(C∗)nT_n = (\mathbb C^*)^nTn​=(C∗)n is the diagonal subgroup of GLn\mathbb GL_nGLn​ and is hence a linear algebraic group.

  6. Every smooth curve of degree 333 in P2\mathbb P^2P2 is an algebraic group; these are elliptic curves. People supposedly have studied these before.

The following lemma should be reminiscent of a basic fact about Lie groups.

Lemma:   Any algebraic group GGG is a smooth variety and its (connected or irreducible) components are the cosets gG0gG^0gG0 where g∈Gg\in Gg∈G. Moreover, G0G^0G0 is a closed normal subgroup of GGG and the quotient group G/G0G/G^0G/G0 is finite.

Proof.   The variety GGG is smooth at some point ggg, and hence also at the point ghghgh for any h∈Gh\in Gh∈G since multiplication is a morphism. This implies GGG is smooth everywhere.

Every variety has only finitely many connected components. Since GGG is a disjoint union of distinct cosets gG0gG^0gG0 and each of these is connected, they form the connected components. The inverse ι(G0)\iota(G^0)ι(G0) is a connected component since ι\iotaι is an isomorphism of varieties, and because eGe_GeG​ is in both G0G^0G0 and ι(G0)\iota(G^0)ι(G0) it must be that ι(G0)=G0\iota(G^0) = G^0ι(G0)=G0. For any g∈G0g\in G^0g∈G0 we then know that g−1∈G0g^{-1} \in G^0g−1∈G0, and hence both gG0gG^0gG0 and G0G^0G0 contain eGe_GeG​. This means gG0=G0gG^0 = G^0gG0=G0 and hence G0G^0G0 is a closed subgroup of GGG. Likewise, gG0g−1=G0gG^0g^{-1} = G^0gG0g−1=G0 so G0G^0G0 is normal.

The group G/G0G/G^0G/G0 is equal to the number of cosets of gG0gG^0gG0, and we have already argued that there are finitely many of these.

Actions on Varieties

A GGG-variety is a variety XXX equipped with an action of the algebraic group GGG

α:G×X→X, (g,x)↦g⋅x\begin{aligned} \alpha:G\times X\to X, ~ (g,x) \mapsto g\cdot x \end{aligned}α:G×X→X, (g,x)↦g⋅x​

This result motivates the following definition, which is what led me to these notes in the first place.

A rational GGG-module is a complex vector space VVV (possibly infinite dimensional) equipped with a linear action of GGG such that every v∈Vv\in Vv∈V is contained in a finite-dimensional GGG-stable subspace on which GGG acts algebraically.
