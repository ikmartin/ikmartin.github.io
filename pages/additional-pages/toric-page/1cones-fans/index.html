Definition of Toric Variety

Recall from the primer that (C×)n=Spec⁡C[x1,...,xn]x1x2...xn(C^\times)^n = \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{C}[x_1,...,x_n]_{x_1x_2...x_n}(C×)n=SpecC[x1​,...,xn​]x1​x2​...xn​​ is called the algebraic torus. Qualitatively, toric varieties are varieties over C\mathbb{C}C which 1. contain a copy of (C×)n(\mathbb{C}^\times)^n(C×)n and 2. whose geometry is largely determined by the torus. Here is a definition:

Definition: A toric variety is a variety XXX together with an open dense subset T⊆XT \subseteq XT⊆X isomorphic to (C×)n(\mathbb{C}^\times)^n(C×)n for some nnn such that the natural action T×T→TT\times T\to TT×T→T extends to an action T×X→XT\times X\to XT×X→X of TTT on XXX.

Some authors, including Fulton, require that toric varieties be normal. This is because the category of normal toric varieties is equivalent to the category of rational polyhedral fans, and the study of toric varieties is really about moving between these two categories. By omitting the normal hypothesis we get a slightly weaker correspondence, discussed later.

Example 1: (C×)n(\mathbb{C}^\times)^n(C×)n is of course itself an affine toric variety. The natural torus action is simply coordinate wise multiplication and the corresponding ring map is given by comultiplication:

T×X→X;(t1,...,tn)⋅(s1,...,sn)↦(t1s1,...,tnsn)T\times X\to X\hspace{1em};\hspace{1em}(t_1,...,t_n)\cdot (s_1,...,s_n)\mapsto (t_1s_1,...,t_ns_n)T×X→X;(t1​,...,tn​)⋅(s1​,...,sn​)↦(t1​s1​,...,tn​sn​) C[t1±,...,tn±]→C[y1±,...,yn±]⊗CC[y1±,...,yn±];ti↦xi⊗yi\mathbb{C}[t_1^{\pm},...,t_n^{\pm}] \to \mathbb{C}[y_1^{\pm},...,y_n^{\pm}]\otimes_{\mathbb{C}}\mathbb{C}[y_1^{\pm},...,y_n^{\pm}]\hspace{1em};\hspace{1em}t_i \mapsto x_i \otimes y_iC[t1±​,...,tn±​]→C[y1±​,...,yn±​]⊗C​C[y1±​,...,yn±​];ti​↦xi​⊗yi​

Example 2: Cn\mathbb{C}^nCn is an affine toric variety, where the torus action T×X→XT\times X\to XT×X→X is again given by pointwise multiplication on coordinates.

Example 3: PCn\mathbb{P}^n_{\mathbb{C}}PCn​ is a toric variety. The torus is T={[z0:...:zn] ∣ zi≠0}T = \left\{[z_0:...:z_n] ~\mid~ z_i\neq 0\right\}T={[z0​:...:zn​] ∣ zi​=0} and the torus action is again given by pointwise multiplication, this time on homogeneous coordinates.

It should be remarked that a toric variety is actually a package

(X,T→openX,T×X→X)(X, T \xrightarrow{\text{open}}X, T\times X \to X)(X,Topen​X,T×X→X)

consisting of the variety itself, a dominant open embedding of the torus and a specified torus action. There are typically many choices of embeddings and actions for any given toric variety, so it is standard practice to fix a choice of each before doing anything else.

N=Hom⁡Grp(C×,T).N = \operatorname{Hom}_{\text{Grp}}(\mathbb{C}^\times, T).N=HomGrp​(C×,T).

There is one other thing I'd like to point out here. Notice how there are three "2-dimensional" components of the fan, three "1-dimensional" components and one "0-dimensional" component namely the origin. These components are called cones, and their dimension relates to the dimension of their torus orbits in the following way. If σ\sigmaσ is a ddd-dimensional cone with a corresponding point PσP_{\sigma}Pσ​, then T⋅PσT\cdot P_{\sigma}T⋅Pσ​ is a dim⁡X−dim⁡σ\operatorname{dim} X - \operatorname{dim} \sigmadimX−dimσ subspace of XXX. In our case, the orbits of the points corresponding to the 2-dimensional cones are just points, the orbits of the 1-dimensional cones become copies of PC1\mathbb{P}^1_{\mathbb{C}}PC1​ and the action of TTT on the origin recovers the entire torus. This phenomenon will be explored further when we discuss the "orbit cone correspondence".

Rational Polyhedral Cones

I hope that you are now adequately convinced that fans connect in some interesting way to toric varieties. In this section, we'll cover the basics of the polyhedral geometry needed to understand this connection.

Fix a lattice N≅ZnN \cong \mathbb{Z}^nN≅Zn and let NR=N⊗ZR≅RnN_{\mathbb{R}} = N \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{R}\cong \mathbb{R}^nNR​=N⊗Z​R≅Rn. We first define polyhedral cones:

Definition: A convex polyhedral cone in NRN_{\mathbb{R}}NR​ is a set

σ={a1v1+...+asvs∈NR ∣ ai≥0}\sigma = \{a_1v_1+...+a_sv_s \in N_{\mathbb{R}} ~\mid~ a_i \geq 0\}σ={a1​v1​+...+as​vs​∈NR​ ∣ ai​≥0}

generated by any finite set of vectors v1,...,vs∈NRv_1,...,v_s \in N_{\mathbb{R}}v1​,...,vs​∈NR​. Such vectors are called the generators of σ\sigmaσ, and we sometimes write spanR≥0(v1,...,vs)\text{span}_{\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}}(v_1,...,v_s)spanR≥0​​(v1​,...,vs​) to denote σ\sigmaσ. In addition, σ\sigmaσ is said to be

  • rational if each generator has integer coordinates, i.e. if vi∈Nv_i \in Nvi​∈N for all 1≤i≤s1\leq i\leq s1≤i≤s

  • strictly convex if σ\sigmaσ does not contain a line The dimension of σ\sigmaσ is denoted dim⁡σ\operatorname{dim} \sigmadimσ and is defined to be the dimension of R⋅σ=σ+(−σ)\mathbb{R} \cdot \sigma = \sigma + (-\sigma)R⋅σ=σ+(−σ) as a linear space over R\mathbb{R}R.

Note that the word "polyhedra" refers to the finite generating set of σ\sigmaσ: this assumption disallows cones which look like ice cream cones.

The final fact we will need is the following:

Fact: If σ\sigmaσ is a rational convex polyhedral cone then σ∨\sigma^\veeσ∨ is a rational convex polyhedral cone.

This is not difficult to prove; one can easily find a procedure by which to find generators for σ∨\sigma^\veeσ∨ given the generators of σ\sigmaσ and use this to prove both rationality and convexity. We refer the reader to page 12 of Fulton's book.

This gives us Gordan's lemma, which will be critical to our construction of toric varieties from cones.
